Thesis

For my Thesis research, I returned to some of the papers about digital Nomads that I received in Week 3. I originally wanted to look at digital nomads as a means of exploring the idea of working anywhere and quickly realised that it had some interesting parallels with the experience of working from home where lines between life and work become blurred. I read a paper called ‘The Freedom Trap’ which focused in on the illusion of freedom that digital nomads seek which ironically results in more difficulty separating life from work. The digital Nomads have to go to great lengths to create a distinction between the two and in some cases end up working longer and more intensely from the pressure to maintain their brand. The digital Nomads are often forced to intensely monitor time in order to find time for their own life. It looked at how digital Nomads are dissolving boundaries between travel, work and leisure by going to tourist spots and trying to work in them. The digital Nomads often romanticize the lifestyle making some interviews less reliable as they tend to focus on the positive aspects. The paper did an Ethnographic study which surprisingly led to disciplining strategies being an integral part of the results which was a surprise to the Nomads and the investigator.

The article research started to identify that digital Nomads indirectly seek out structure and found that they couldn’t escape some of the tropes and values of the conventional office space. Shared office space were often considered an essential aspect for digital Nomads to be productive, creating a physical boundary between work and leisure as well as opting to primarily use Laptops over mobile devices as a less distracting method of working. There was a sense that productivity is of the upmost importance and structure was an essential part of achieving this. It pointed out that working late or into the evening was what they aimed to avoid but this wasn’t always the case based on workload and colleague time zones. The paper also noted a taboo around taking calls in the public workspace and that although Digital Nomads opted for this public workspace, they would often avoid interaction and would prioritise workspaces where they weren’t sitting across from others. The paper also found that some digital nomads that were travelling and working alone experienced a strong sense of isolation and that when alone it was often harder to find delineation between work and leisure. They found that couples worked with a better sense of structure and work life division as the partners understood one another’s rhythm and would often intervene if they felt the other was working too hard and reminded them to take a break. They also noted that Nomads often changed their view on deadlines after time on the road, understanding the sense of structure that it can create. This is something the more entrepreneurial Nomads couldn’t enjoy, requiring self-imposed time management techniques. Some used a wide variety of project management techniques, including Agile,Footnote3 SprintsFootnote4 and timeboxing.Footnote5. They paper noted the lengths that digital nomads went to in order to avoid distractions with one participant having gotten a programmer to write a system that would lock him out of certain apps until he had put down 4.5 hours of work.

Thesis

I completed and submitted my Ethics application this week. Most of my thesis work at the time was being done via the workshop preparation. I was investigating AI in the workplace and looking at different ways AI was being used to improve worker efficiency, or in many cases, take the work of humans. I was learning about the augmented workforce which is the collaboration of humans and AI which is intended to reduce administrative workload. This is often suggested in a positive way to be a means of improving worker efficiency and not take jobs. Unfortunately, I couldn’t see past the fact that these technologies will reduce workload of individuals to the point where companies may require less staff.

In the context of working from home, I found articles that discussed the boom in worker surveillance software since the beginning of the Covid-19 pandemic which forced many people to work from home. This software tracks keystrokes, time spent in front of the screen, emails sent and read, time spent on phone and more. It gathers this data and ultimately assigns a productivity score. I didn’t feel positively about this type of software and was disappointed to read that many of these companies had increased customer bases by over 40%. It was an example of some of the more intrusive ways that technology was being exploited in the workplace. This was a common trend amongst my research related to AI. During the debate, Ayoola made a very good point that I hadn’t specifically thought of. She was debating against the idea of AI being positive for humanity and mentioned that sometimes in a workplace you have people that elevate those around them, creating a good work atmosphere that naturally improves morale, comradery and workplace culture. An AI like the examples above would never capture these human elements and if it came down to cutting costs, may remove choose to let these people go. It further reinforced my feelings that these types of software aren’t positive for the work environment.

Thesis

The Thesis topic that I had put forward was investigating ways of improving the working from home experience. This was something that I was personally motivated by as I had gone through the stark change from office life to working from home that Covid lockdown caused. This left the impression on me that the experience could be enhanced. There were aspects of working from home that I liked and some that I did not and so I hoped that I might be able to research and design something that tipped the scales in favor of working from home. I could see the benefits socially and environmentally of working from home and while my ideal future would be a hybrid of office and working from home, it is something I see in our future. At this point we were still waiting to find out who our Thesis supervisors would be.

Proposal:

In my research/design, I want to explore the current experience of working from home. Many have been thrust into working from home with no preparation and although many have proven to be able to work effectively, there is potential for enhancing the experience. I’d like to understand the challenges and limitations that people are facing. I want to design a product that improves and potentially broadens the experience in ways that weren’t possible in an office environment. The general research/design problem is:

How can the working from home experience be improved?

Some of the ideas I have in this area already are

  • A product that acts as a workspace in any location, supporting the idea of ‘Work Anywhere’ rather than ‘Work from Home’
  • Creating a more visceral and engaging meeting experience, considering gamification within a meeting/workshop context.
  • Portable note taking apparatus and software that cuts down on paper use.

    • I plan to do online research on different work environments that have been able to work from home. I will be interviewing people working in different departments in order to gather information on their experience. These will include workers in the tech industry and banking. Using the feedback, I’ll look for common trends and issues faced to understand where the main pain points are for working from home as well as considering new opportunities that working from home poses. Depending on the type of product being designed, I may use solid works to design the early physical prototype and if there is a digital interface, I’ll use either sketch or Figma to design. I want to research this area because I see it as an unavoidable change in our future. I’m welcome to the idea of working from home becoming more common, but I also feel that it’s lacking and can be a frustrating experience. This change has been and will continue to be positive for the environment with less need for public transport and relieving the pressure on overpopulated urban spaces. I’ve experienced the positive and negative sides of working from home and I think it’s an interesting area to assess with a focus on user experience. I’m also well positioned to research this as many of my friends have unexpectedly gone from an office environment to working from home as well as experiencing this myself. Rather than try and make the home an office, I think working from home could be evaluated as something new in its own right.
